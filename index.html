⚙️ Methodology
How We Compute the NPWEI
A four-step pipeline from raw satellite data to a normalised population exposure index
1
Data Acquisition
Monthly TROPOMI NO₂ L3 files (.nc4) downloaded from NASA GES DISC for Jan 2020 – Dec 2024. WorldPop 2020 population raster clipped to West Africa at 0.1° resolution.
2
Urban Masking
Pixel density computed accounting for latitude-varying cell area. Only pixels with ≥50 people/km² contribute — isolating genuine urban exposure from sparse rural areas.
3
PWE Computation
PWE = Σ(population × NO₂) / Σ(population) per city and country. Seasonal means computed for DJF, JJA, and Annual using RegularGridInterpolator alignment.
4
Normalisation & Export
Raw PWE normalised 0–100 per season: NPWEI = (PWE − min) / (max − min) × 100. Results exported as JSON and PNG overlay tiles for web rendering.

⚠️ Risk Classification
Understanding the NPWEI Risk Scale
The Normalised Population-Weighted Exposure Index (NPWEI) is scored 0–100 across five tiers. Cities and countries are benchmarked relative to each other within each season, making the index a comparative measure of relative urban NO₂ burden rather than an absolute concentration threshold.

🏛 Research Team
Built by KNUST's Climate & Health Research Lab
This platform was developed by the Climate and Health Research Lab (CAHL) at the Kwame Nkrumah University of Science and Technology (KNUST) in Kumasi, Ghana. CAHL investigates the intersection of climate variability, air quality, and population health across sub-Saharan Africa — producing open-access tools for evidence-based policy dialogue.
📡
Satellite Data Pipeline
Monthly TROPOMI L3 NO₂ columns processed with Python (xarray, RegularGridInterpolator) and overlaid with WorldPop urban masks at 0.1° resolution.
🌐
Open Access Commitment
All processed NPWEI data is freely available for download. The platform is designed for researchers, policymakers, and public health practitioners across West Africa.
